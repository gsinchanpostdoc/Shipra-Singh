I am a postdoctoral ecologist specialising in forest ecosystem services and plant functional ecology in the Western Himalayas. My current work at IIASA focuses on understanding how forest ecosystem services respond to environmental gradients and management scenarios across altitudinal and climatic ranges in the Himalayas — integrating field data, trait-based modelling, and ecosystem service frameworks.

My doctoral research at Jawaharlal Nehru University examined how plant functional traits and soil microbial communities respond to disturbance intensity, slope aspect, and elevation in Western Himalayan temperate forests. I combine phytosociological field surveys with multivariate ordination, metagenomics, and process-based vegetation modelling (Plant-FATE).

I am fluent in R, Python, ArcGIS, and QGIS, and have contributed to international fieldwork, the BES and AGU meetings, and the Ecosystem Services Partnership Conference 2024 in Wageningen.

Current work & published findings

Research

My current IIASA project asks: How do forest ecosystem services respond to environmental gradients and management scenarios in the Himalayas? This involves decomposing management-based scenarios and identifying conservation priorities across elevational, climatic, and disturbance gradients.

My doctoral work established that low-to-moderate disturbance intensity does not significantly alter species composition or functional traits in Western Himalayan temperate forests, but crown cover (CC) and specific leaf area (SLA) shift with disturbance — indicating a trade-off between conservative and acquisitive resource strategies.

In my latest Ecological Indicators (2025) paper, structural equation modelling reveals that evergreen shrub traits are tightly constrained by topography and stand canopy cover, while deciduous traits respond more indirectly via vegetation and soil feedback loops — explaining 63% of vegetation structure and 50% of trait variation across 1500–3000 m asl.

Figure 1. PCA biplot of twelve plant functional traits for six dominant shrub species, coloured by functional type (deciduous: orange; evergreen: green). Deciduous species cluster around acquisitive traits (SLA, LPC, LNC, LKC, HT, CC); evergreens align with conservative traits (LDMC, LT, LA, LRWC, Leaf pH). PC1 + PC2 explain 39.8% of total trait variance. Ellipses show 95% confidence intervals.

After Singh & Verma (2025). Ecological Indicators, 178, 114000. Fig. 4.

Figure 2. Structural equation model (SEM) for evergreen shrubs showing direct and indirect pathways from Topography and Stand Canopy Cover to Trait Expression (PC1: LT, LDMC, SLA). Topography (β=–0.92) and Canopy Cover (β=+0.99) together explain 63% of vegetation structure variation. Trait expression R²=0.50. Arrow thickness is proportional to path coefficient magnitude.

After Singh & Verma (2025). Ecological Indicators, 178, 114000. Fig. 5b.

Figure 3. Violin plots showing variation in specific leaf area (SLA), crown cover (CC), and microbial biomass carbon (MBC) across three disturbance intensity classes in temperate forests of western Himalaya. SLA increases with disturbance (acquisitive shift), CC decreases due to lopping, and MBC is highest in undisturbed (LDI) plots reflecting intact litter and carbon accumulation. All differences significant at p < 0.001 (one-way ANOVA).

After Singh (2021). Frontiers in Forests and Global Change, 4, 710658. Fig. 2.
